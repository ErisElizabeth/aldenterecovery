A Gentle Account of Recovery, Al Dente

Foreword: On Being Al Dente

There is a school of recovery that believes you must be boiled all the way down. Cooked soft. Rendered helpless in a rolling pot until whatever you were before dissolves into the broth and you emerge, at last, agreeable and mushy and grateful. It calls this humility. We have watched it call a lot of things humility.

We propose something else. We propose that a noodle is at its best when it is al dente; cooked enough to hold together, tender enough to bend, and still, unmistakably, itself. You do not have to be reduced to paste to be well. You do not have to announce that you are raw, ruined, and beyond saving before anyone will pass you the colander. You can simply be done enough. Firm at the center. Ready.

This little book is a parody, and it is also completely sincere, which is the only combination we trust. It pokes fun at the rooms that ask you to declare yourself powerless at the door, that hand you a Higher Power like a coat-check ticket, that turn a person's hardest season into a script of slogans everyone recites and no one questions. We have sat in those rooms. Some of us were helped there and left anyway. This is for the ones who left, and for the ones still deciding.

The Flying Spaghetti Monster, may His noodly appendages be forever warm, asks very little of us. He does not require that we be broken first. He would, on the whole, Really Rather We Didn't be so hard on ourselves.

Eris' Story

I hit my wall on an ordinary day. I will spare you the tale of how I got there; you have your own, and mine would only flatter us both into thinking it was special. It wasn't. It was the usual arithmetic of a person subtracting herself a little at a time and calling it a rough patch.

So I went to a meeting, because that is what you do. I sat in the folding chair. I drank the coffee that tasted like the inside of a thermos. And when it came around to me, I was told (kindly, warmly, by people who meant every word) that the first thing I had to do was admit I was powerless. That I was, at my core, defective, and that the plan was to stay defective forever and simply not act on it, one day at a time, world without end.

I want to be fair to those rooms, because they save lives. But I remember sitting there thinking: I have spent years believing I was broken. That belief is not the cure for my problem. It is my problem. You are asking me to take the thing that is drowning me and make it my foundation.

I didn't drink that night. But I didn't go back either. And for a while I was nowhere: no rooms, no slogans, no map. Just me and a lot of evening.

What filled the evening, absurdly, was a free coding tutorial. I don't fully know why. I think I wanted a problem that would tell me plainly whether I'd solved it or not; no sponsor's frown, no reading of the room, just a green check or a red error. I wrote my first program. I don’t do anything half-assed, I skipped over Hello World and built a journaling app. Then a game, a website, another game, an ARFID companion.

Here is the thing nobody told me about the compiler: it does not shame you. It does not sigh. When you get it wrong it says (with total indifference) exactly what is wrong and where. Then it waits, until the heat-death of the universe if that is what is required, for you to try again. There is no moral verdict in a stack trace. There is only: not yet, here's why, go on. I had never in my life been corrected without contempt. I did not know it was possible. A machine taught me.

The small wins stacked up like clean dishes. A loop that ran. A bug I chased for three days and finally cornered at 2 a.m., alone, sober, laughing out loud in my room at a sober house. Each one was a tiny proof that I could set an intention and follow it to the end: that I was not, in fact, powerless. I was under-practiced. Those are extremely different diagnoses.

And I wasn't as alone as I'd feared. I found forums where strangers answered my beginner questions without once asking me to confess anything. I found people who fixed my broken code for the pure joy of fixing broken things, and who let me do the same for others the moment I was able. That turned out to be the medicine: not being watched, but being useful. Not being fixed, but fixing alongside.

I stayed sober. I am staying sober. Not because I made peace with being defective, but because I stopped believing I was. The noodle held its shape.

What We Found Instead

We are not against help. We are against a particular flavor of help that asks you to swallow your own self-respect as the price of admission. When we set the shame down, three things were underneath it, waiting the whole time.

Self-Directed Autonomy

You are the author of your recovery. Not powerless: under-practiced, which is a condition that improves with practice. A compiler will let you run the same broken function a thousand times without ever once telling you that you are the problem; it assumes, generously, that you are a competent person who has not yet found the bug. We suggest you extend yourself the same assumption. Set a small intention. Follow it to the green check. Do it again tomorrow. Sobriety compiles the same way software does: not in one heroic surrender, but in a great many tiny commits.

Mutual Aid

Nobody debugs alone forever. But there is a world of difference between a room that requires your confession and a community that just wants your pull request. We found the second kind and it healed us. Ask your beginner questions without apologizing for them. Answer someone else's the moment you can, you will be more qualified than you think, and helping is not a reward you earn after you are well; it is one of the ways you get well. Open-source your recovery. Fork someone's good idea. Merge freely.

Pleasure & Self-Compassion

The old model treats joy with suspicion, as if you have to stay a little miserable to stay safe. We think that's backwards. The Flying Spaghetti Monster is not a deity of self-denial. Treat your errors like a stack trace, not a sin: information, not indictment. Rest is not relapse. A good day is not a warning sign. You are allowed to enjoy the life you are staying sober for. In fact, enjoying it is a fairly important part of the plan.

May your center stay firm and your edges stay kind.

May you be corrected without contempt and helped without a toll.

May you never again mistake being reduced to paste for being made well.

You are not broken. You are al dente.

Pass the sauce.

R’amen.
